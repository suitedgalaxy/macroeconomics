Units 1 & 2: Economic Indicators & The Business Cycle

All countries have three macroeconomic goals:

  1. Promote economic growth
  2. Limit unemployment
  3. Limit inflation

The Circular Flow Model

Factor Payments- payment for the factors of production: rent, wages, interest, and profit.
Transfer Payments- the government redistributes income (e.g. welfare, social security).
Subsidies- government payments to businesses.

The Production Possibilities Curve and The Business Cycle

The production possibilities curve shows the trade-offs in the ways an economy can allocate its resources. It graphically demonstrates scarcity, trade-offs, opportunity costs, and efficiency. Any point along the curve represents a possible combination that can be produced given a certain productivity or efficiency. The curve is not fixed — an increase in efficiency or productivity will shift the curve outward and vice versa.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the dollar value of all final goods and services produced within a country in one year. Final means that GDP only counts new goods and services.

Measuring Growth

Measuring Standard of Living

GDP Per Capita is GDP divided by the population. It identifies on average how many products each person makes. GDP per capita is the best measure of a nation's standard of living.

GDP Exclusions

  1. Intermediate Goods
      2. Goods inside final goods don't count. The price of a finished car is included in GDP, not the stock radio or tires.
  2. Nonproduction Transactions
      3. Financial Transactions (e.g. stocks, bonds, real estate)
      Used Goods (e.g. old cars, used clothes)
  3. Nonmarket and Illegal Activities
      4. Things made at home — household production
      (e.g. unpaid work, black markets, drugs)

Calculating GDP

  1. Expenditures Approach
      2. The sum of all the spending on final goods and services produced. This is the sum of consumer spending (C), business investment (I), government spending (G), and net exports — exports minus imports, or X minus M.
  2. Income Approach
      3. The sum of all income earned from selling all final goods and services produced — Factor Payments. This is the sum of labor, rental, interest, and profit income, earned from performing work, property owned by individuals, loaning money to businesses, and money businesses have after paying all of their costs, respectively.
  3. Value-added Approach
      4. The sum of the dollar value added at each stage of the production process.

Each approach should generate the same number.

The Components of GDP


Consumer Spending — C

  1. Durable Goods- (e.g. washing machines, refrigerators, cars)
  2. Non-Durable Goods- (e.g. food, clothes, toilet paper)
  3. Services- (e.g. dental work, repairs, tutoring)

Business Investment — I

The word "investment" is used differently in economics. Investment is never when individuals buy assets like stocks and bonds. Investment is always when businesses buy capital like machines, resources, and tools.

Does the purchase of a new oven produced in America count as consumer spending or investment spending? It depends. It is consumer spending if the oven is purchased for a home and investment spending if the oven is purchased by a business.

Government Spending — G

Government expenditures tracks the spending made in the "public sector." This includes payments made by the government for goods and services. It does not include money spent on transfer payments. It does not include interest payments on the national debt.

Unemployment

Unemployment- workers that are actively looking for a job but aren't working.
The Unemployment Rate- the percentage of people in the labor force who want a job but are not working.


Three Types of Unemployment
  1. Frictional Unemployment
      2. Temporary unemployment or being between jobs. Individuals are qualified workers with transferable skills.
      Seasonal Unemployment is a specific type of frictional unemployment, which is due to time of year and the nature of the job.

  2. Structural Unemployment
      3. Changes in the labor force makes some skills obsolete. These workers do not have transferable skills and these jobs will never come back. Workers must learn new skills to get a job. The permanent loss of these jobs is called "creative destruction." Technological Unemployment is a type of structural unemployment where automation and machinery replace workers.

  3. Cyclical Unemployment
      4. Unemployment caused by a recession. This is sometimes called "demand deficient unemployment."

Frictional and structural unemployment are present at all times because some people will always be between jobs or replaced by technology. The Natural Rate of Unemployment is frictional unemployment plus structural unemployment. This is the amount of unemployment when the economy is healthy and growing. We calculate GDP at Full Employment Output, or at the natural rate of unenployment — which excludes cyclical unemployment — and label it Y.

Criticisms of the Unemployment Rate
Discouraged Workers- some people are no longer looking for a job because they have given up (they no longer count in the labor force thus the unemployment rate falls).
Underemployed Workers- someone who wants more hours but can't get them is still considered employed.
Inequalities- the overall unemployment rate doesn't show disparity for minorities and teenagers.

Inflation

Inflation is the rising of the general level of prices and the reduction of the "purchasing power" of money. When inflation occurs, each dollar will buy fewer goods than before. In general, high inflation is bad because banks don't lend and people don't save. This decreases investment and GDP.

Deflation is the decrease in general prices or a negative inflation rate. Deflation is bad because people will hoard money and assets. This decreases investment and GDP. Disinflation is the decrease of inflation — prices increasing at slower rates. The target number for inflation is 2%.

The Effects of Unanticipated Inflation

Lenders — people who lend money (at fixed interest rates) — and people with fixed incomes and savers are hurt by inflation.
Borrowers — people who borrow money (at fixed interest rates) — are helped by inflation.
Nominal Wage- wage measured by dollars (rather than purchasing power).
Real Wage- wage adjusted for inflation.
